UNIT 2: Curriculum Models

2.1 Meaning of Curriculum Models

Curriculum models are systematic frameworks that guide planning, development, implementation, and evaluation of curriculum. They help educators decide:

  • What should be taught
  • How it should be taught
  • How learning should be assessed

A curriculum model provides:

  • A logical structure for curriculum design
  • A theoretical foundation for decision-making
  • Alignment of objectives, content, teaching, and evaluation

2.2 Purpose of Curriculum Models

  • Bring clarity and organization
  • Ensure alignment of goals and activities
  • Promote consistency in teaching
  • Support instructional decision-making
  • Facilitate curriculum evaluation

2.3 Major Curriculum Models

2.3.1 Tyler’s Objective Model

Proposed by Ralph Tyler (1949). It emphasizes:

  • Setting educational objectives
  • Selecting learning experiences
  • Organizing learning experiences
  • Evaluating outcomes

Strength: systematic and goal-oriented. Limitation: too linear and rigid.

2.3.2 Taba’s Grassroots Model

Proposed by Hilda Taba (1962). Steps include:

  • Diagnosis of needs
  • Formulation of objectives
  • Selection & organization of content
  • Selection & organization of learning experiences
  • Evaluation

2.3.3 Wheeler’s Cyclical Model

Wheeler viewed curriculum as a continuous cycle where evaluation feeds back into planning. This model emphasizes curriculum as dynamic.

2.3.4 Miller and Seller’s Models

  • Transmission Model – teacher-centered
  • Transaction Model – learner-centered
  • Transformation Model – holistic and humanistic

2.4 Strengths and Limitations

Strengths:

  • Provide guidance for curriculum design
  • Improve coherence and alignment
  • Support logical decision-making

Limitations:

  • Some models are inflexible
  • Overemphasis on objectives reduces creativity
  • Do not always address cultural or contextual issues

UNIT 3: Curriculum Implementation

3.1 Meaning

Curriculum implementation is the process of putting a planned curriculum into practice through teaching and learning activities.

3.2 Importance

  • Ensures curriculum objectives are achieved
  • Aligns teaching strategies with curriculum goals
  • Ensures learners benefit from planned learning experiences
  • Determines success of educational reforms

3.3 Factors Influencing Implementation

3.3.1 Teachers

Teacher competence, attitudes, and motivation strongly influence implementation.

3.3.2 Learners

Learner readiness, interest, and learning styles shape implementation.

3.3.3 Teaching Resources

Availability of textbooks, digital tools, and infrastructure is essential.

3.3.4 School Leadership

Supportive leadership improves supervision and instructional quality.

3.3.5 Policy & Environment

Educational policy, culture, and societal expectations influence implementation.

3.4 Challenges

  • Teacher resistance
  • Lack of training
  • Limited resources
  • Overloaded curriculum
  • Poor supervision

3.5 Strategies for Effective Implementation

  • Teacher professional development
  • Adequate resources
  • Learner-centered approaches
  • Strong leadership
  • Continuous monitoring & evaluation

UNIT 4: Curriculum Evaluation

4.1 Meaning

Curriculum evaluation is the systematic assessment of a curriculum’s effectiveness, relevance, and ability to achieve intended objectives.

4.2 Purpose

  • Assess achievement of objectives
  • Identify strengths and weaknesses
  • Improve curriculum quality
  • Guide decision-making and policy

4.3 Types of Evaluation

4.3.1 Formative Evaluation

Conducted during implementation to improve learning.

4.3.2 Summative Evaluation

Conducted at the end of a program to assess effectiveness.

4.4 Evaluation Models

4.4.1 Tyler’s Model

Focuses on measuring achievement of objectives.

4.4.2 Stufflebeam’s CIPP Model

Evaluates Context, Input, Process, Product — supports decision making.

4.5 Challenges

  • Lack of expertise
  • Limited data
  • Stakeholder resistance
  • Resource constraints
