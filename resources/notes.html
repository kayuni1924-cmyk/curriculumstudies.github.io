UNIT 2: Curriculum Models

2.1 Meaning of Curriculum Models

Curriculum models are systematic frameworks that guide curriculum planning, development, implementation, and evaluation. They help educators determine:

  • What should be taught
  • How it should be taught
  • How learning should be assessed

2.2 Purpose of Curriculum Models

  • Bring clarity and organization to curriculum development
  • Ensure alignment between goals and instructional activities
  • Promote consistency and coherence in teaching
  • Support evaluation and improvement processes

2.3 Major Curriculum Models

2.3.1 Tyler’s Objective Model

  • Define objectives
  • Select learning experiences
  • Organize learning experiences
  • Evaluate achievement

Criticism: Too linear and rigid.

2.3.2 Taba’s Grassroots Model

  • Diagnosis of learner needs
  • Formulation of objectives
  • Selection & organization of content
  • Selection & organization of learning experiences
  • Evaluation

2.3.3 Wheeler’s Cyclical Model

Views curriculum development as continuous and cyclical.

2.3.4 Miller & Seller Models

  • Transmission Model — teacher-centered
  • Transaction Model — learner-centered
  • Transformation Model — holistic + humanistic

2.4 Strengths & Limitations

Strengths:

  • Provide guidance for curriculum development
  • Ensure systematic approach
  • Promote coherence

Limitations:

  • Some models lack flexibility
  • May neglect creativity and innovation
  • May not fully address cultural/contextual issues

References

  • Tyler, R. W. (1949).
  • Taba, H. (1962).
  • Ornstein & Hunkins (2018).
UNIT 3: Curriculum Implementation

3.1 Meaning

Curriculum implementation refers to the actual use of the curriculum in teaching and learning.

3.2 Importance

  • Ensures curriculum objectives are achieved
  • Connects curriculum plans with classroom practice
  • Promotes effective learning experiences

3.3 Factors Affecting Implementation

  • Teachers: competence, motivation, attitudes
  • Learners: readiness, interests, learning styles
  • Resources: textbooks, technology, infrastructure
  • School Leadership: supervision, support
  • Policy Environment: curriculum guidelines, societal expectations

3.4 Challenges

  • Limited resources
  • Inadequate teacher training
  • Resistance to change
  • Poor supervision

3.5 Strategies for Effective Implementation

  • Professional development for teachers
  • Provision of adequate resources
  • Learner-centered pedagogy
  • Effective leadership
  • Continuous monitoring and evaluation

References

  • Fullan, M. (2007).
  • Print, M. (1993).
  • Marsh, C. J. (2009).
UNIT 4: Curriculum Evaluation

4.1 Meaning

Curriculum evaluation measures the effectiveness, relevance, and impact of a curriculum.

4.2 Purpose

  • Determine achievement of objectives
  • Identify strengths and weaknesses
  • Support curriculum improvement
  • Guide educational decision-making

4.3 Types of Evaluation

  • Formative: during implementation
  • Summative: after implementation

4.4 Evaluation Models

  • Tyler's Model: measures objective achievement
  • CIPP Model: Context, Input, Process, Product

4.5 Challenges

  • Lack of reliable data
  • Limited expertise
  • Resistance from stakeholders
  • Resource constraints

References

  • Stufflebeam & Coryn (2014)
  • Posner (2004)
  • Ornstein & Hunkins (2018)
